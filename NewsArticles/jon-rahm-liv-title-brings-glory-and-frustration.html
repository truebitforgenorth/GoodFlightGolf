Winless Champion: Jon Rahm’s $18 Million LIV Title Brings Glory and Frustration

⏱ 5 min read | Posted 08/24/2025

Jon Rahm clinched the 2025 LIV Golf individual season championship on Sunday in Indianapolis, earning the league’s $18 million bonus prize. The Spaniard’s consistency across the season proved decisive in edging Joaquín Niemann, who won five tournaments but finished second in the standings.

Rahm’s path to the title came without a single tournament victory. In the finale, he delivered a brilliant closing round of 11-under 60 to force a playoff with Sebastian Muñoz. But the drama was short-lived: Muñoz birdied the first extra hole to claim his maiden LIV trophy, while Rahm settled for another runner-up finish.

Despite the defeat, Rahm’s steady accumulation of points throughout the season—four second-place finishes and multiple top-10s—was enough to secure the season-long crown. It was his second straight LIV title, though this one carried a very different emotional weight.

Bittersweet Triumph
Speaking after the round, Rahm admitted the victory came with conflicted feelings. “It’s a great moment,” he said, pausing with a wry smile. “But it just doesn’t feel great to finish the year losing two playoffs. Niemann probably was the more deserving guy to win it.” His words reflected what many were already thinking. Niemann had been the face of victory in 2025, hoisting five trophies and leading the tour in prize money with over $22 million in event earnings. Rahm, by comparison, earned about $13.6 million on the course, boosted into near parity only by the $18 million season-long bonus.

The points-based system crowned Rahm champion, but it also sparked debate: should relentless consistency outweigh outright dominance? Or, as some observers suggested, did Rahm’s title expose a flaw in LIV’s structure?

A Season of Almosts
Rahm’s year can be summed up in moments of brilliance that ended just shy of glory. His 60 in Indianapolis was one of the lowest rounds of the season, a fiery reminder of why he’s considered among the world’s best. But like earlier close calls, it didn’t end with him lifting the trophy. “It’s slightly bittersweet,” Rahm said. “I know what it means to win this, but I’ll always remember the playoffs I didn’t finish.”

Still, the season-long honor is far from meaningless. Rahm now joins rare company as a back-to-back individual champion on LIV, his résumé fortified by consistency that few could match. In a league built on big-money prizes and bold narratives, Rahm’s 2025 story may be the most complex of them all: a season without a victory, but a championship worth $18 million.
