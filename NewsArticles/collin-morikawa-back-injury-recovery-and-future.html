Collin Morikawa's Back Injury: Recovery, Augusta Grit, and What's Next

5 min read | Posted April 16, 2026

Collin Morikawa's spring 2026 story turned fast. On March 12, 2026, he withdrew from THE PLAYERS Championship after just one hole, saying the problem hit during a practice swing on the 11th tee. What initially looked like a short interruption quickly became one of the bigger health storylines heading into Augusta.

The next key date was March 31, 2026, when Morikawa also pulled out of the Valero Texas Open. That mattered because San Antonio was supposed to be his final tune-up before the Masters. Golf Channel reported that his agent, Andrew Kipper, said Morikawa had been doing productive rehab at home, but that week was still too early for a return. That update suggested recovery was moving forward, just not on the original timeline.

By Masters week, the conversation had shifted from pure pain to trust. ESPN reported on April 6, 2026 that Morikawa was taking things day by day and that imaging had not shown anything worse than what he had dealt with before. Three days later, both ESPN and PGA TOUR described a more revealing picture: Morikawa said he had never felt that nervous before a round, not because of leaderboard pressure, but because he still did not fully trust how his body would respond once competition started.

That makes his Masters finish even more interesting. After opening with a 74, Morikawa played his final three rounds in the 60s and finished tied for seventh at 9-under. Associated Press via ESPN described him as hobbled at times, but he still closed with back-nine momentum and called the week one he would remember for the strength it took to keep believing he could get through it. That does not mean the issue is fully behind him, but it does show that his floor is still very high even while managing discomfort and lingering hesitation.

What Recovery Looks Like Right Now

Based on the public reporting available as of April 16, 2026, the most encouraging sign is that Morikawa was able to complete a major championship week after the two withdrawals. The less encouraging sign is that several reports described the problem as more than a one-day flare-up. The physical symptoms appeared tied to back spasms and residual discomfort, while the mental side centered on whether he could swing freely without expecting another setback.

That combination matters for a player like Morikawa because his game depends so heavily on rhythm, balance, and elite approach control. Even if the pain level drops, hesitation alone can change speed, strike quality, and decision-making. In that sense, the Masters may have been less of an all-clear and more of a proof of concept: he can compete at a high level while recovering, but the recovery process may still require careful workload management.

Short-Term Future

As of April 16, 2026, PGA TOUR materials for the RBC Heritage list Morikawa in the field. That is the next strong indicator to watch. If he can go from Augusta straight into another high-level start without another withdrawal, the tone around this injury changes from immediate alarm to ongoing maintenance. If he has to limit preparation again or pull back mid-event, the concern shifts right back to durability.

The reasonable read, based on the reporting above, is that Morikawa's outlook is still positive but not fully settled. His game is clearly good enough to contend when he is even close to full trust. The next question is whether he can stack normal tournament weeks again without the back issue dictating his schedule.
